Black Tech Workers Face Little Upward Mobility in Small CitiesT

The U.S. TechHire Designation Hasn't Helped

By Sherrell Dorsey

A look at private sector employment data derived from the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission reveals that these majority minority cities, even with the TechHire charge, are still facing tremendous barriers to entry for African Americans within the information sectors. But independent, private-sector jobs found in much more robust startup communities are hard to come by in smaller cities where industries like manufacturing and agriculture have historically been the largest economic drivers.

Jackson, MI Stands Out

Surprisingly, Jackson, Mississippi, was a stark outlier. Compared to the other majority-minority TechHire cities, over the course of eight years, the metro-area had a positive growth rate of African Americans employed in the information sector of over 3 percent steadily outpacing its TechHire compatriats who each saw a decline in employment year after year.

This Isn't Exactly Progress

On the surface, Jackson can seem like a success story. A closer look at Jackson's largest employers reveals the truth about what kinds of information-jobs African American employees earn their living For those employed in the private sector, these information jobs are mostly in service-based roles at large companies like telecommunications companies like AT&T and Verizon—some of the city’s top employers outside of government and hospital IT departments.

Compared to the other majority-minority TechHire cities, the Jackson metro area saw a positive growth rate of over 3 percent increase of African Americans employed in private industry. Jackson’s increase outpaced its TechHire compatriots who each saw a decline.


Another Look At Jackson

Jackson, with a population of fewer than 200,000 residents, less than 30 percent of whom holding a bachelor’s degree or higher in a science, technology, engineering, or math degree, was named a TechHire city in 2016. Participation in the campaign is completely voluntary but encourages community groups, workforce development agencies, economic development departments, and private sector employers to establish concrete goals to create training programs and partnerships to route residents to tech-centered jobs in a variety of occupations.
